1. Human Workflow Specification (Gold Standard): Richmond et al. (2020) Realist Review
- Human Reviewer Architecture and Algorithm
1.1 Objective (what the human workflow is trying to achieve)
Richmond et al. (2020) conduct a realist review to build an explanatory programme theory of
how educational interventions develop analytical and non ‑analytical clinical reasoning in
undergraduate health professions education, explicitly addressing why interventions work, for
whom, when, and under what circumstances.
● Evidence (Abstract): “This realist review examines the way educational interventions
develop analytical and non-analytical reasoning ability in undergraduate education…
seeking not only to identify if an intervention works, but also understand the reasons
why, for whom, and in what circumstances.”
● Evidence (Abstract): “The aim of this study is to develop understanding… when they
do, for whom and in what circumstances.”
● Evidence (Methods): “Realist research data are analysed and interpreted to form context,
mechanism, outcome configurations (CMOCs) and collectively form a programme
theory.”
This specification “unpacks” the human reviewer workflow that produced the paper’s final
conclusions, i.e., the sequence of reasoning operations, decision rules, and intermediate products.
2. Human “System Architecture” (who does what, with what tools, producing what
artefacts)

responsibilities and quality control.

2.3 Core artefacts
The workflow produces and iteratively refines the following artefacts:
● Aim + Research Questions (the controlling specification of what the review must
explain)
● IPT (Initial Programme Theory) (a theory seed that defines what counts as relevant
evidence)
● Search specification (databases, year constraints, and theory ‑derived search
concepts/terms)
● Screening/appraisal decisions (inclusion rules tied to theory contribution and
methodological rigour)
● CMOC set (coded configurations across included papers)
● Cross‑study pattern map (recurrent CMOC patterns and their consolidation into key
contexts/mechanisms)

● Final programme theory (results + figures + conclusions expressing “what works, for
whom, when, why”)
3. Human Algorithm (step ‑by‑step operations, decision rules, and iterative loops)
Stage 0 — Problem framing: define realist explanatory intent and formal research
questions
Input
● The educational and safety importance of developing clinical reasoning.
● The observed problem that interventions may not work uniformly across learners and
contexts.
Operation (human reasoning)
● Establish a realist explanatory aim (mechanism ‑ and context‑sensitive causation).
● Derive explicit research questions from the aim.
Output
● A stated aim plus two research questions guiding the entire process.
Evidence (Introduction)
● Aim is explicitly articulated, followed by:
“The research questions that emerged from this aim were: (i) What educational interventions are
effective…?, and (ii) When and why are they effective, for whom and in what circumstances?”
Stage 1 — IPT construction (theory seeding): create the initial explanatory model before
large‑scale screening
This stage is the primary “human import” step: humans define the conceptual target that later
governs search and inclusion.
Input
● Scoping/background literature
● Expert opinion and researcher experience
● Team expertise as clinical teachers

===== PAGE 4 =====
● Complementary learning theories to structure how “context” will be sought
Operation (algorithmic sequence)
1. Background/scoping searches are conducted (by AR).
2. The team builds consensus on what kinds of educational interventions promote analytical
vs non‑analytical reasoning.
3. Outputs from steps (1)–(2) are used to draft initial CMOC hypotheses.
4. After research‑team feedback, these drafts are consolidated into an Initial Programme
Theory (IPT).
5. Additional learning theories are integrated to define “context levels” to be investigated
(student, teacher, learning encounter/activity, organisational).
Output
● IPT + initial CMOC drafts + a context ‑level lens that directs later searching and
synthesis.
Evidence (Abstract/Methods)
● “Literature from a scoping search, combined with expert opinion and researcher
experience was synthesised to generate an initial programme theory (IPT).”
● “AR developed consensus amongst the research team…”
● “The outputs… led to the development of initial drafts of CMOCs… Following
feedback… an IPT was constructed…”
● “Other relevant theories were also integrated into the IPT… Cognitive flexibility
theory… Situativity theory…”
Stage 2 — Search strategy and execution: theory ‑driven retrieval, with deliberate term
expansion
Input
● IPT themes/constructs (the theory seed determines what to retrieve)
Operation (algorithmic sequence + loop)
1. Conduct structured searches in MEDLINE, PsycINFO, ERIC, and CINAHL (May 2017),
incorporating IPT themes.

===== PAGE 5 =====
2. Apply a time boundary: searching begins in 2000, justified by the 1999 “To err is human”
report.
3. Perform a supplementary search to add additional terms as initial searching identifies
relevant constructs (pattern recognition, deliberate practice, illness scripts, knowledge
acquisition/recall).
4. Operationalise “knowledge” as a multidimensional construct (Table 1) to support
consistent reasoning about “low/high knowledge” contexts.
Output
● A candidate corpus for screening and appraisal.
Evidence (Methods 2.1)
● Database list, search date, year constraint, and supplementary terms are explicitly stated.
Stage 3 — Screening and appraisal: select studies for theory contribution and assess
methodological credibility
This stage formalises the key realist inclusion principle: studies are included because they
contribute to theory building, not merely because they match a topic.
Input
● Candidate corpus from Stage 2
Operation
1. Title/abstract screening assesses relevance; studies are retrieved if they are deemed to
contribute to theory building.
2. Full‑text review assesses methodological rigour as credibility/trustworthiness.
3. Apply the review’s operational definition of “educational intervention” for full ‑text
decisions.
4. Include studies describing a technique that can be integrated into an intervention (not
only fully packaged interventions).
5. Search reference lists of included papers for additional theory ‑contributing studies.
Output
● The final included set that will drive programme theory refinement.

Stage 4 — Data extraction and CMOC coding: convert each included paper into structured
causal explanations
Input
● 28 included full texts
● IPT and CMOC framing
● Data extraction forms
Operation (algorithmic sequence + inference rules)
1. For each included full text, human reviewers devise CMOCs.
2. Apply realist inference rules for latent constructs:
● Mechanisms (especially cognitive/emotional learner responses) may be implicit and
therefore are theorised or inferred from learning theory and study data.
● Outcomes may also be theorised when not explicit.
1. Quality assurance:
● AR performs initial CMOC coding.
● A second reviewer checks all 28 articles for consistency.
1. Manage and structure coded information:
● NVivo is used for storing texts and coding contexts.
● Excel is used to further elaborate which contexts affect mechanisms and outcomes (i.e.,
to refine causal mapping).
Output

● A CMOC set across all included studies, with coded contexts and structured links

Important representation detail
Richmond’s Results present CMOC statements using a C / Mresource / Mresponse / O structure
(e.g., in Context 1), reflecting their operational decomposition of mechanisms into resource and
response in reporting.
Stage 5 — Cross ‑study synthesis and iterative refinement: derive recurrent CMOC
patterns and consolidate into programme theory
This stage is where the final conclusions are produced: human reviewers aggregate and reconcile
CMOCs across studies, iteratively revisiting earlier papers as theory evolves.
Input
● CMOC set across studies + IPT
Operation (pattern‑finding + iterative loop)
1. Compare studies and identify recurrent patterns of CMOCs.
2. Recognise that different studies contribute differently (some highlight contexts, others
mechanisms).
3. Iteratively re‑analyse earlier studies in light of theories arising from later studies.
4. Maintain a theory ‑building stance (rather than hypothesis testing) due to under ‑reporting
or distance of outcomes.
5. Produce consolidated outputs: key contexts and key mechanisms determining
effectiveness.
Output

4. Public, verifiable outputs (what Richmond reports that can be used as gold ‑standard
targets)
These are the observable outputs that a verification effort can directly target:
● Included set size and flow: 149 full texts retrieved → 28 included (25 + 2 + 1).
● Programme theory expressed via CMOCs: CMOCs collectively form the programme
theory.
● Five key contexts (student level) explicitly enumerated in Results:
● low knowledge / low domain knowledge / inability to apply knowledge
● high domain-specific knowledge
● positive coping strategies / appropriate self-confidence/self-efficacy
● negative coping strategies / low self-confidence/self-efficacy
● different levels of knowledge within a group
● High‑level conclusions about what is required to develop reasoning (knowledge
acquisition/recall + clinical experience + practice in real/simulated environments) and
how contexts (pre-existing knowledge, self-confidence) condition effectiveness.
5. What this specification clarifies
This document makes explicit how Richmond’s team produced the paper’s conclusions:
● They did not start with “28 papers” arbitrarily; they built an IPT first, then used it to
guide search and inclusion.
● Inclusion decisions were theory ‑contribution driven and filtered by rigour.
● They transformed papers into CMOCs using a realist inference rule that allows
theorising/inference for mechanisms/outcomes when implicit.

● They produced conclusions via cross ‑study pattern identification and an iterative
re‑analysis loop, culminating in a programme theory expressed through five key contexts
