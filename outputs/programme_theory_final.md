## Initial Programme Theory (IPT)
If educational interventions are designed to engage both analytical and non-analytical reasoning processes, then undergraduate medical and healthcare students will develop improved clinical reasoning abilities, leading to enhanced diagnostic accuracy and more robust illness scripts.

The evidence suggests that these instructional strategies are particularly beneficial for students with limited clinical experience, as they provide a structured framework for reasoning and decision-making (S001, S002, S004).

The convergence of evidence from multiple studies (S002, S004, S005, S006, S008, S009) suggests that instructing students to utilize both analytical and non-analytical reasoning approaches is a robust strategy for improving diagnostic accuracy and learning outcomes in medical education. This programme theory highlights the critical role of instructional design in supporting novice learners to build upon their existing knowledge and develop effective clinical reasoning skills.

## PTS4 — Students with negative coping / lacking self-efficacy (E05)

Programme Theory Statement 4 (PTS4) explores the impact of negative coping strategies and a lack of self-efficacy among students (E05) on their learning outcomes, particularly in the context of absent, incomplete, or erroneous feedback (E14). This context is characterized by students' challenges in navigating information during patient interactions and their lack of confidence in diagnostic reasoning (S001, S005, S010). The absence or inadequacy of feedback (E14) exacerbates these challenges, leading to confusion (E45) and ultimately resulting in poor illness script development (E36) and negative learning outcomes (E38) (S001, S005, S007, S009, S010, S011, S014).

A consistent pattern emerges across multiple studies, demonstrating that students with negative coping strategies or low self-efficacy (E05) experience confusion (E45) when feedback is insufficient or flawed (E14), which hinders their ability to develop effective illness scripts (E36) (S005, S007, S009, S010, S011, S014). This demi-regularity is observed in 14 studies, underscoring the critical role of feedback in supporting students' diagnostic reasoning and learning processes.

The negative impact of inadequate feedback is further highlighted by the finding that students often retain incorrect initial diagnoses and struggle to improve their diagnostic accuracy without proper guidance (S009, S010). Additionally, the absence of feedback limits students' ability to generate self-explanations, a mechanism crucial for deepening understanding and enhancing clinical reasoning skills (S014).

Boundary conditions are evident in the presence of negative CMOCs, which consistently indicate that the lack of effective feedback in environments where students already struggle with self-efficacy leads to suboptimal educational outcomes. This suggests that interventions aimed at improving feedback quality and fostering self-efficacy could mitigate these negative effects and enhance learning outcomes.

In conclusion, PTS4 highlights the detrimental effects of inadequate feedback on students with negative coping strategies or low self-efficacy, emphasizing the need for targeted interventions to support these learners in developing robust diagnostic reasoning skills and achieving positive educational outcomes.

## PTS5 — Students with different levels of knowledge within a group (E06)

Programme Theory Statement 5 (PTS5) explores the impact of heterogeneous knowledge levels among students (E06) on learning outcomes when exposed to clinical reasoning training (CRT) interventions. The studies within this PTS consistently demonstrate that when students with varying levels of knowledge engage with CRT, where an expert's reasoning processes are explicitly revealed and discussed (E07), or when they listen to near-peers think aloud using prompts and examples (E17), they are able to build upon their existing knowledge (E39) and develop more complete illness scripts (E43) (S003, S021, S024).

In the context of CRT, the explicit revelation and discussion of an expert's reasoning processes (E07) provide students with insights into clinical decision-making, which is particularly beneficial for students with diverse knowledge levels (E06). This intervention facilitates the analysis of doctor-patient interactions, revealing repeated cycles of questions and answers (E19), which students appreciate and find relevant to their learning (E22) (S003). Similarly, when students engage in self-explanation while solving clinical cases, it triggers monitoring processes (E19) that activate their biomedical knowledge, allowing them to create new cognitive links and develop coherent case representations (E43) (S021).

The evidence suggests that these interventions are effective in fostering a positive learning experience (E22) and enhancing diagnostic accuracy through the development of complete illness scripts (E43) (S003, S021, S024). However, the studies also highlight the challenge of ensuring sufficient plurality in educational experiences, which is crucial for maximizing the benefits of CRT in diverse student groups (S024).

Overall, the convergence of findings across studies indicates that CRT interventions, whether through expert modeling or peer-led self-explanation, are beneficial for students with varying knowledge levels. These interventions enable students to build upon their existing knowledge and develop comprehensive illness scripts, ultimately leading to improved diagnostic accuracy and a positive learning experience.
